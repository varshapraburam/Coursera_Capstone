Introduction

CHAPTER-4 PROFILE OF CHENNAI CITY

 

Chennai has become an important destination for employment and visiting the attractions, in recent years. It has evoked as a city with tremendous potential for industrial growth because of its economic viability and available infrastructure. The State Government Departments set off towards increasing trade and commerce links with other countries and also developing greater industrial growth. The whole boom in the trade and business of Chennai city accounts largely for its rapidly growing population. More businesses and job opportunities in the city lure people from in and around Chennai and settle in here. Major multinationals have already set up their branch offices in Chennai, leading to more people being hired by these companies. Thus, this chapter deals with socio economic profile of Chennai City which comprising of population growth, infrastructure development and industrial development.

Location

 

Chennai has grown from a small village named Madraspatnam or Chennaipatan. The name Chennai has thus a historical root and the city was renamed “Chennai” in 1996 by an Act of 1996, namely the Alteration of Name Act. The structure of the city approximates a semi-circle with extensions into the interior in five directions, North, South, West, Northwest and Southwest.

 

MAP.1.CHENNAI CITY

 

A small fishing village at the time of British colonization, the transition of Chennai has evolved over centuries into a town, a city and metropolis and it covers a total population of 4.5 million. The Chennai Urban Agglomeration (CUA) comprises of the city plus 4 municipalities, 5 townships, one cantonment and 38 villages. The extent of the CUA is 531sq.km. The population of the CUA in 2001 of the Chennai Metropolitan Area (CMA) accounted for an area of 1176 sq.km includes the CUA along with 227 villages which have been set aside for special planning consideration and political activities besides being a living place for a greater proportion of the population of the CMA.

Size and Growth of Population

 

Chennai grew in stages both in land area and population. Accessible data indicate that the population of the city was growing very slowly until the end of the second decade of the twentieth century. However, since 1921 the growth of population quickened by 1941but it saw a tremendous population increase. In fact, by 1941 Chennai had emerged as a principle metropolis enjoying the best of words, urban amenity and rural atmosphere.

It can be noted that Chennai City had a population of 3.97 lakh in 1871, which rose to 37.95 lakh in 1991; indicating more than nineteen times increase in population during the twelve decades. In 2001, Chennai City had a population of about 45 lakh. The 1991 census shows that the pace of growth of population in Chennai City during1981- 91 was not as rapid as in the earlier decades of 1951 to 1981. However, the density of the population has shown a steady and stable increase over the years. The growth of

 

population has shown a steady and stable increase over the years. The growth of population in Chennai City started to gain momentum only since 1921. In 1921 – 31, for the first time, the population of the city marked an absolute increase of more than 1.2 lakh over the previous decade with growth a rate of 22.83 percent. The main reason for the growth was the inflow of migrants, besides acceleration in natural growth rate.

In 1931-41, the rate of growth of population showed a slight fall as compared to the previous decade though the addition to the total population was considerable. During this period, the growth of population was not much impressive even in the face of expansion of the geographical area of Chennai City from 68.17 sq.km to 77.21 sq.km. The relatively slow pace of growth of population might be attributed to the low rate of migration into the city.

Since 1941 the city experienced a great increase in population, which was due to the influx of migrants, the fail in death rate and jurisdictional adjustments of the city limits. During 1941 – 51, the city‟s population registered the highest growth rate of 82.13 percent with an absolute increase of 6.38 lakh. This can be attributed to three reasons:

v  The expansion of city limit from 77.21 to 128.83 sq.km. by incorporating        the adjoining suburban areas in south and west of Chengalpet District; the large influx of migrants from other parts of the country, and

v  Increase in natural rate of population growth.

 

The 1951 census recorded a high growth of population. The subsequent decade of (1951 – 61) recorded a low growth compared to the previous one. This might be due to the slight reduction in the inflow of migrants. In 1961 -71, the growth of population further increased to 42.81 percent with an addition of population to 7.40 lakh. The growth of population was due to rise in the inflow of migrants and increase in natural growth rate. In 1971 -81, the rate of growth of population was 32.69 percent which was less than the previous period. In 1981 -91, the rate of growth of population was 15.8 percent which was less than that of the previous period decade. And in 1991 – 2001 the rate of growth of population was 16.7 percent, which is 0.9 percentage point more than the rate of growth of population in the previous decade.

Demography

 

The demographic components such as birth, death and migration determine the growth of population. The population in Chennai City has  been influenced by both natural increase and immigration. Natural increase of population is the excess of births over deaths or the difference between fertility and mortality. The successful reduction of death rates by the advancement of medical facilities had a direct impact on the growth of population in CMA. The Health Department of the Corporation of Chennai has been taking various steps, which include, among others, compulsory vaccination, training of midwives, and the provision for basic pre-parental and post-parental services to mothers, clinics, besides programmes.

Migration

 

A considerable proportion of population growth in Chennai City attributed to the influx of migrants. However, available evidences indicate that immigration has been a steady case of population growth. Since 1921 the city of Chennai has received a net migrant population of 1.6 million persons, majority of them from rural areas of Tamil Nadu. Intercity and inter- state migrants account for a meager proportion of the total migrants.

Both push and pull factors acted for the steady growth of migrants, in and around Chennai City. Frequent failure of monsoon in the region caused mass exodus of agricultural labourers and marginal farmers into Chennai City from the adjoining districts. Prevalence of widespread unemployment, poverty, lake of opportunities, social disparities and discrimination in rural areas and small urban areas acted as push factors. Among the pull factors are the new industries that were set up in and around Chennai City, particularly since the era of planning. As a result, a large number of migrants come to the city are from various places.

A number of public undertakings like the Integral Coach Factory (ICF) at perambur, Madras Refineries Limited (MRL), Madras Fertilizers at Ennore. The Tank Factory at Avadi and the expansion of the harbour spurred industrial activities immediately after Independence attracted a large number of migrants. Density of Population in Chennai City

 

Increasing concentration of population in a specific land area is a concomitant process of urbanization. However, the rapid growth of population and therefore the density of population have led to several urban ills such as overcrowding, exorbitant rise in rent and land values, mushrooming slums, sanitary living conditions and shortage of civic amenities.

Though the density of population continued to increase form decade to decade, the index of the growth recorded sporadic increase only after 1951. The high degree of concentration of population received temporary respite whenever the city limits were expanded. As a consequence of the

 

expansion of the city limits, the average number of persons per sq.km gets reduced. It is quite obvious that the density of population per sq.km. in Chennai City was 3.2 times the density of Chennai Urban Agglomeration and

6.44 times the density of Chennai Metropolitan Area during 1971, However, in 1981 the respective growth was times that of the Chennai Urban Agglomeration and 4.89 times higher than the Chennai Metropolitan Area. The Importance of Slum Upgrading

Upgrading of slums requires a wider improvement plan consisting of sanitary and livelihood programmes besides housing. Redesigning and re- constructing settlements with the participation of colonists and assistance from public bodies, is a viable option with the least amount of disturbances of the settlers or their livelihood. This method of slum improvement needs to be practiced on a much wider scale. VAMBAY projects permit up-gradation. There are many Central government schemes such as NSDP, SJSRY, VAMBAY, Night-Shelter, 2- Million housing scheme, AUWSP for the slum important.

They include identification of Urban poor, formation of community groups, involvement of NGOs, self-help/thrift and credit activities, training for livelihood, credit and subsidy for economic activities, housing and sanitation, environment, convergence of service, etc., though there are many central government schemes, the state governments have a larger role play in slum improvement and clearance such as; State Governments should carry out requisite amendments to the municipal laws; Urban Poverty Alleviation cells

 

should draw up City Poverty Alleviation Plans; tasks of various agencies should be effective; thrust should be on provision of all basic services to the slum settlements, such as potable water and sanitation services, including household taps, toilets with septic tanks, covered drains, waste collection services etc. Other activities for socio-economic improvement of the slum populations should also be taken up.

In slum environment the poor are exploited and ignored rather than supported. There is a major role for the State and its agencies, including the Urban local bodies in the provision of services such as water supply, health, education, sanitation, legal protection, employment generation, etc., to take care of the needs of the poor. This is a challenge because of the known weakness of the system, such as lack of accountability, corrupt practices, and the dominant role of the more powerful groups in urban governance.

The increase in population and the resulting intensified land use has led to a high concentration of urban dwellers in slums and squatter settlements. Heaps of garbage and potholes are common sights while stray dogs; pigs, flies and mosquitoes are rampant. The growth of urban areas has generated a new range of disease-producing agents in the form of chemical residues, toxic wastes, car exhaust fumes and various synthetic products. The majority of slums suffer from scarcity of water, and the water available is often contaminated. Most of the poor are dependent on public sources of water supply.

 

Personal sanitation in the slums is also dismal. The community was poorly maintained, lacked water supplies, overcrowded, and suffered from poor or non-existent drainage. People did not often build private latrines because of limited space. There were no facilities to clean up this human waste in any slum. Garbage, a serious problem in cities, assumes a different dimension

This metropolis is often called the cultural capital of India for its deep- rooted traditions and long heritage. Chennai is a city younger than its image. More than any other city in India, it is a true reflection of this country‟s diversity. In a time span of just over 350 years, Chennai has blossomed into a charming city that has a large heart and is very welcoming. It is city that encourages all forms of development, both modern technology and the traditional arts and crafts, and it embraces a series of paradoxes. It was in 1639 that Francis Day and Andrew Cogan, agents for the English East India Company, acquired a strip of land on lease from the Vijayanagar King. They built the Fort St.George, which remains of the city‟s important landmarks and serves as the Government Secretariat today. It was built to set up a factory that served as a nucleus for British settlements that began to be formed. Surrounding villages like Triplicane, Purasawalkam, Egmore and Chetput slowly merged with the new developments, to form Chennapatnam, as it was known. The city was called Madras till 1996 and then renamed Chennai. Today this buoyant metropolis is a blend of the old and the new, the traditional and the modern.

Contribution of migration to urbanization

 

Migration is one of the important factors contributing to the growth of urban population. The total urban population of the country, excluding

 

Jammu and Kashmir increased from 217.6 million in 1991 to 283.6 million in 2001 registering a growth rate of 30.3 per cent. The migration data of 2001 Census indicates that 20.5 million people enumerated in urban areas are migrants from rural areas who moved in within the last 10 years. There are

6.2 million migrants who have similarly migrated from urban areas to rural areas. Thus the net addition to urban population on account of migration is

14.3 million. This works out to be 6.6 per cent of the urban population in 1991. In other words, out of the urban growth of 30.3 per cent, 6.6 per cent is accounted for by migration to urban areas.

It is observed that there is significant increase in role of participation of women migrants in Chennai City. The chapter helps to analyse the sample respondents with available socio economic information of the women migrants.
